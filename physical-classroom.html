Fostering Student Discourse

How can I arrange my physical classroom?

Whether it’s an auditorium filled with rows facing a stage or a restaurant with cozy booths, different settings suggest, support, and impede certain behaviors. Our classrooms are no different! And while we all have some real constraints, we can think about how our choices align with our expectations for student activities. In this conversation, you'll consider how your classroom space can encourage different types of student interactions.

A teacher considers a variety of desk arrangements for a particular lesson.

  • The ingredients a teacher has to work with (like class size, furniture, room construction) vary, so the implementation of the arrangements shown might look different…what are the *key features* of each arrangement?
  • Imagining yourself in the shoes of a student, what does each of these physical arrangements suggest about the work you’ll be doing?
  • How do these different arrangements support or hinder student to student discourse? What about independent student thinking?
  • Even within a single class period, the way a teacher wants students to engage changes many times (e.g. going from working independently, to small groups, to whole groups, debates, etc.) When would you need physical shifts (i.e. moving desks) to support these transitions, and how do you support the transitions without physical shifts?
  • What is your vision for student discourse? What possible changes or tweaks might you make to your physical space to move towards that vision?
  • What factors do you consider when you rearrange your physical classroom space?
  • To prepare your students for any changes in the physical classroom space, how would you communicate and manage student expectations?
  • What is one small thing you can do tomorrow in support of those shifts?
