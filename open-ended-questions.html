Focusing on the Real World

How can I strengthen students' responses to open-ended questions?

Real-world lessons often lead to big open-ended questions -- ones in which students may draw on mathematical analysis as well as experiences, beliefs, and opinions to construct responses that are entirely their own. While there's not a single right answer, some are clearly stronger than others. In this conversation, you'll explore how to make sense of and strengthen student responses to real-world questions that do not have a single right answer.

  • Strong responses to open questions typically make a clear claim, include evidence and/or reasoning, acknowledge the counterargument, and consider the audience or perspective. Pick one of the two pages in the Spark Media to review. What are some strengths and weaknesses in each response?
  • What can be challenging for students about these open questions? What can be inviting? Consider students from across the spectrums of achievement and readiness in your math class.
  • What follow-up questions could a teacher use in the moment to nudge students to make one of these weak responses stronger?
  • As a teacher, how should your approach to "open" questions be different than it is for questions with one right answer?  What should be the same?
  • What skills and mindsets do students need to engage with questions without right answers in math class?
  • What activities, practices, etc. do you already do to build and reinforce those skills and mindsets over time? What new things could you try?
  • What is one small thing you can do tomorrow in support of those shifts?
