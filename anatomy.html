Anatomy of a Convo

Every convo is built from the same three ingredients: a one-page Convo Guide, a piece of Spark Media, and a real-world math lesson that anchors the whole thing. Here's a closer look at each.

The Convo Guide

The Convo Guide is a single page. It's the agenda for the discussion — six to eight carefully crafted questions, organized into the four sections (Observe, Discuss, Relate, Commit), with suggested timing for each.

The questions aren't designed to have a single right answer. They're designed to surface different perspectives, get teachers comparing what they actually do in their own classrooms, and end somewhere productive. The instructions for running the convo are printed right on the guide, so there's no separate facilitator's note, no slide deck, no "host packet." Everyone at the table holds the same piece of paper. The page is the agenda. The agenda runs itself.

Every participant should have their own printed copy. The convo works best when people can mark the guide up — circling questions, jotting reactions, writing down their own commitment at the end.

The Spark Media

The Spark is what gives the convo something concrete to look at. It's a short artifact — usually an animated video, sometimes a transcript of a classroom conversation, sometimes a set of student work samples — that depicts a single instructional moment.

Most of the videos are animated rather than filmed in real classrooms. That's a deliberate choice. Real classroom footage is rich, but it's also noisy — there's always more going on than the one thing you're trying to discuss. Animation lets the Spark isolate a single move, a single decision, a single pattern, without all the surrounding context distracting from the point.

And to be clear: the Sparks aren't portrayals of excellent teaching. They're not aspirational, and they're not cautionary. They're conversation starters — vivid, specific, and just ambiguous enough that two thoughtful teachers can disagree about what they're seeing.

The third ingredient

Why Citizen Math lessons are the perfect anchor

Every Spark is set inside a real Citizen Math lesson — and that's not a small thing. The whole reason these convos work is that Citizen Math lessons are doing something most math curricula don't even attempt.

A typical math lesson teaches students to apply a procedure to a problem and get to a right answer. There's nothing wrong with that. But it doesn't produce the kind of classroom that's interesting to talk about. A lesson where everyone gets the same answer the same way is a lesson where there's not much to discuss in the teacher's lounge afterward.

Citizen Math lessons are different. They build problems around real questions — should small-footed people pay less for shoes, where should homeless shelters go, how should stores be allowed to price items on sale — and ask students to bring genuine mathematical analysis to bear on them.

Three things make these lessons land:

  • They're mathematically rigorous. Real math, used carefully. Not "math-flavored" — the kind of problem-solving where students actually have to model, reason quantitatively, and defend their thinking.
  • They're conversational. Students don't just present their answers. They listen to each other, build on each other, and disagree with each other. Talking about math is the point.
  • They're focused on the real world. Most "real-world" math just dresses procedures up in a context. Citizen Math does the opposite — it uses math to actually understand the world. The questions are ones a reasonable person would want answered.

That combination is what makes Citizen Math lessons perfect for these convos. Every interesting teaching problem in this collection — how to handle a "right" answer that wasn't the point, how to navigate a rabbit hole, how to support a sensitive conversation, how to strengthen a student response to an open-ended question — only exists because a Citizen Math lesson created the conditions for it. A lesson with one right answer doesn't produce rabbit holes. A lesson without real-world context doesn't surface sensitive moments. A lesson without genuine discourse doesn't generate student-to-student dynamics worth talking about.

Each convo is paired with a specific Citizen Math lesson as its focus. You don't need to have taught that lesson to use the convo. But you'll get more out of it if you take five minutes to skim the lesson before you sit down — and you'll almost certainly want to teach it afterward.
