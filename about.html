About the Convos

A small set of resources built on a simple idea: the richest professional learning happens when teachers are given the time, space, and structure to talk with one another — and then someone has the good sense to get out of the way.

What this is

Convos with Colleagues is a collection of eleven roundtable discussion guides for math teachers. Each one is designed for two to five educators, runs thirty to sixty minutes, and needs no facilitator. There are no slides to advance, no speaker at the front of the room, no prep beyond skimming a focus lesson and printing a one-page guide. Just a thoughtful set of questions, a piece of media to spark the discussion, and the colleagues you already trust.

The convos are the kindling. The fire is yours.

Where these came from

Convos with Colleagues was developed by the team at Citizen Math with support from an Education Innovation and Research (EIR) grant from the U.S. Department of Education. The goal was deceptively simple: build ongoing professional development for teachers using Citizen Math lessons — a kind of lesson that's mathematically rigorous, conversational, and focused on the real world.

We started by identifying the instructional practices that characterized great teaching of these lessons, alongside the questions and challenges teachers reported most often. And then we prototyped. A lot.

We considered in-person PD sessions — too expensive and too hard to scale. We built online courses for teachers to work through on their own — some called them thought-provoking, but the experience was lonely. We tried cohort calls with teachers across the country, facilitated by our director of professional learning — and the insights were real, but something felt off. The teachers were the ones with the expertise. Why was everything running through the facilitator?

So we tried something we hadn't quite been brave enough to try yet: we didn't show up.

We crafted a small set of questions, dropped them in front of a group of great teachers, and got out of the way. We hosted that first session online — barely warning the teachers that they'd be facilitating themselves — and it was magic. Educators across the country were swapping resources, riffing on each other's ideas, saying things like "oh wow, I never thought of that" and "that gives me a totally different perspective."

Then we tried it in person, with a group of teachers in New York City. At the end of a day of traditional PD, we gave them the new format. Some knew each other; most didn't. There was a moment of awkwardness — wait, who's running this? — and then they were off. In a room of nearly fifty people, everyone was talking. It got loud. The district leaders were beside themselves: You're telling me we can hand these out, with no prep, and still get conversations like this? Yes.

From there we refined the format, slimmed everything down to a single-page agenda, baked the instructions and timing right into the guide so the conversation could truly run itself, and developed convos around the eleven instructional questions teachers told us mattered most. This site is the result.

The four-part structure

Every convo follows the same shape: Observe, Discuss, Relate, Commit. The structure isn't arbitrary. It draws on two well-established threads in adult learning theory.

The first is experiential learning, popularized by educational theorist David Kolb in the 1980s. Kolb's model holds that adults learn most deeply through a cycle: encountering a concrete experience, reflecting on it, generalizing from it, and then experimenting with what we've learned. Observe and Discuss live in the reflection-and-generalization halves of that cycle. Relate and Commit live in the experimentation half.

The second is the broader field of andragogy — the study of how adults learn, distinct from how children do — most associated with Malcolm Knowles. A core principle is that adults learn best when new ideas are anchored in their own experience and when they have agency over what they do next. So the convos don't tell teachers what to do. They invite teachers to observe carefully, generalize together, connect new ideas to their own classrooms, and choose — for themselves — one small thing to try tomorrow.

That last move is the one that matters most. A great conversation that ends without a commitment is just a great conversation. The Commit step is what makes this professional learning rather than a book club.

What's in each convo

A Convo Guide. One page. A small set of carefully crafted questions, organized into Observe, Discuss, Relate, and Commit, with suggested timing for each section. The guide is the agenda. Every participant should have their own copy.

Spark Media. A short video, classroom transcript, or piece of student work that grounds the discussion. Most of the videos are animated rather than filmed in real classrooms — not because real classrooms aren't valuable, but because animation lets us isolate and illustrate a single move or challenge without all the other noise. The Sparks aren't portrayals of excellent teaching. They're excellent kindling.

Lesson Context. Each Spark is contextualized in a specific Citizen Math lesson — a real-world math lesson that's mathematically rigorous, conversational, and focused on the world outside the classroom. You don't need to teach the lesson to use the convo, but skimming it beforehand adds a useful layer.

How to use these

The convos are designed to fit into real life. A few patterns we've seen work:

For school leaders, PLC facilitators, and coaches. Schedule one convo per month. Pick the topic ahead of time so people can come prepared. Build in a brief check-in at the start of the next session about how the previous month's "one small thing" actually went. That follow-up is where the conversation becomes a practice.

For individual teachers. Grab a colleague — or two, or four — and pick a convo that speaks to whatever you're chewing on right now. You don't need permission from anyone. A great convo can happen over a planning period, at a coffee shop after school, or in the corner of a faculty lounge.

The cost

These convos are free. Use them, share them, run them in your school, fold them into your coaching practice. We'd love to know how they go — but we don't need to.
