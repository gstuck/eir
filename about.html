About the Convos

A small set of resources built on a simple idea: the richest professional learning happens when teachers are given time, space, and structure to talk with one another — and then someone has the good sense to get out of the way.

What this is

Convos with Colleagues is a collection of eleven roundtable discussion guides for math teachers. Each one is designed for two to five educators, runs thirty to sixty minutes, and needs no facilitator. No slides, no presenter, no prep beyond skimming a lesson and printing a one-page guide. Just a small set of carefully crafted questions, a piece of media to spark the discussion, and the colleagues you already trust.

The convos are the kindling. The fire is yours.

Where these came from

Convos with Colleagues was developed with support from an Education Innovation and Research (EIR) grant from the U.S. Department of Education. The brief: build ongoing professional development to support teachers using Citizen Math lessons.

Conventional PD didn't fit the bill. In-person workshops were expensive and hard to scale. Self-paced online courses felt lonely. Even facilitated cohort calls had a strange quality — the teachers were the ones with classroom expertise, but every conversation ran through the facilitator anyway.

So we tried something we hadn't quite been brave enough to try yet: we didn't show up.

We wrote a small set of questions, dropped them in front of a group of great teachers, and got out of the way. The first session was online, the teachers were barely warned they'd be running things themselves, and it was magic. Educators across the country were swapping resources, riffing on each other's ideas, saying things like "oh wow, I never thought of that."

Then we tried it in person, with fifty teachers at the end of a long PD day in New York City. After a brief moment of wait, who's running this? — the room was off. It got loud. The district leaders watching were beside themselves: you're telling me we can hand these out with no prep and still get conversations like this?

From there it was a matter of refining: slimming the guide to a single page, baking the instructions and timing into the agenda itself, and developing convos around the eleven instructional questions teachers told us mattered most. This site is the result.

The four-part structure

Every convo follows the same shape: Observe, Discuss, Relate, Commit. The structure isn't arbitrary. It draws on two well-established threads in adult learning theory.

The first is experiential learning, the framework popularized by educational theorist David Kolb. Kolb's model holds that adults learn most deeply through a cycle: encountering a concrete experience, reflecting on it, generalizing from it, and experimenting with what they've learned. Observe and Discuss live in the reflection-and-generalization half of that cycle. Relate and Commit live in the experimentation half.

The second is andragogy — the study of how adults learn, distinct from how children do — most associated with Malcolm Knowles. A core principle: adults learn best when new ideas are anchored in their own experience and when they have agency over what they do next. So the convos don't tell teachers what to do. They invite teachers to observe carefully, generalize together, connect new ideas to their own classrooms, and choose — for themselves — one small thing to try tomorrow.

That last move is the one that matters most. A great conversation that ends without a commitment is just a great conversation. The Commit step is what makes this professional learning rather than a book club.

How to use these

The convos are designed to fit into real life.

For school leaders, PLC facilitators, and coaches. Schedule one convo per month. Pick the topic ahead of time so people can come prepared. Build in a brief check-in at the start of the next session about how the previous month's "one small thing" actually went. That follow-up is where the conversation becomes a practice.

For individual teachers. Grab a colleague — or two, or four — and pick a convo that speaks to whatever you're chewing on right now. A great convo can happen over a planning period, at a coffee shop after school, or in the corner of a faculty lounge.

The cost

These convos are free. Use them, share them, run them in your school, fold them into your coaching practice.
