Tips for a Great Convo

A roundtable discussion designed for two to five educators, thirty to sixty minutes, no facilitator. The format is simple. Doing it well is a small art. A few things we've learned.

01

Someone has to host. No one has to facilitate.

"Hosting" means picking a time and place, picking the convo, sending out the link, and making sure people have printed copies of the guide when they arrive. That's it. Nobody needs to lead the discussion or prepare to be the expert in the room. The Convo Guide is the agenda. Once you sit down, you're all in it together.

02

Ten minutes of prep is plenty.

Before the convo, look over the focus lesson so you have a feel for the mathematical context. Skim the Convo Guide. Jot down a question, a moment from your own classroom, or a recent challenge that's relevant. Print your own copy of the guide. That's the whole prep.

03

Print it.

The Convo Guide is designed to be a paper artifact on the table between you. Marking it up, pointing to a question, annotating your own commitments — those small moves change the texture of the conversation. Reading off a screen isn't the same.

04

Two is enough.

The format works with five. It also works with two. Don't wait for a full PLC, a perfect afternoon, or the right meeting on the calendar. Grab one colleague over coffee and have the convo.

05

Trust the structure.

Observe, Discuss, Relate, Commit. The four sections are sequenced on purpose — observation grounds the discussion in something specific, discussion surfaces ideas, relating brings those ideas into your own practice, and committing turns thinking into action. If a group skips ahead — say, jumping straight into "well, in my class I always…" — gently come back to the question on the page. The structure is doing real work.

06

Watch the clock — a little.

Each section has a suggested time range. Use it as a guide, not a stopwatch. If you're four minutes into Observe and the group is just hitting its stride, give it another beat. If you're fifteen minutes in and nobody's mentioned the Spark, time to move on. Try to leave at least five minutes for Commit. That's the part that matters most.

07

Don't skip Commit.

A great conversation that ends without a commitment is just a great conversation. The "one small thing you can do tomorrow" question is what makes this professional learning rather than a book club. Say it out loud. Write it on the guide. Send it to your colleagues. The smaller and more specific, the better.

08

Bring real curiosity.

You don't have to be an expert to have something worth saying. You also don't have to perform expertise — the convos aren't a stage. The most valuable contributions are usually the most specific: a moment from a class last week, a question you've been turning over, an idea you tried that didn't work. Share generously.

09

Disagreement is a feature.

The questions are designed not to have a single right answer. If two people in the room land in different places, that's the conversation working. Lean into it rather than smoothing it over.

10

Follow up.

If you do these regularly, build in a brief check-in at the start of the next session about how the last "one small thing" actually went. That's where the conversation becomes a practice — and where the practice becomes a culture.
